Abstract
======
This talk presents a bounding gait defined by the composition of several 3D templates to generate steerable and robust running on natural terrain.

Compositions of Templates
======
The bounding gait is defined by the parallel and hierarchical composition of several templates.  At the lowest level, the active damping hopping controller (De and Koditschek, 2018) is mapped to the extension direction of a 3D SLIP model, while a rimless-wheel template is mapped to the swing directions.  This hopping controller energizes the gait, while the rimless wheel template helps each hip track a desired velocity.  Each 3D SLIP module is then wrapped with the anchoring controller from Greco and Koditschek, 2023 and the resulting composition is mapped to the front and rear leg pairs of Spirit.  As in De and Koditschek, 2018, the parallel composition of hoppers in the front and rear of the robot generates a preflexively stable bounding gait.

Results
======

Steering
------

The bounding gait recieves fore-aft and heading velocity targets from a joystick, which are converted into linear velocity targets for the two hips.  The gait responds well to these commands, tracking heading velocity commands up to 1 rad/s and fore-aft velocity commands up to 0.94 m/s (roughly two body lengths per second).

Natural Terrain
------

<!-- Gait works on natural terrain, including gentle slopes.  Slight struggles due to dissipation, especially in thick vegetation.  Steep slopes can cause rollover due to anchoring tracking ground level. -->
Although the gait was tuned for indoor running on flat ground, it handles the variations of natural terrain well without any adjustment, relying only on proprioception.
<!-- It occasionally struggled when climbing steep hills, pushing through dense vegetation, or traversing softer ground, as these conditions dissipated energy faster than it could be replaced by the active damping controller.  We expect that retuning the hopping controller to a higher-energy limit cycle or providing some way for it to adapt to the conditions it encounters would increase the viability of the gait in these conditions.  Additionally, traversing steep slopes introduced a significant offset in roll, leading to occasional failures.  This is a byproduct of the fact that the anchoring controller aligns the robots hips with its toes; if the toes are on slanted ground, the hips will tilt in response.  We expect that adjusting the anchoring to incorporate information from the IMU will enable the robot to correct for this and improve performance on this sort of terrain. -->

Disturbance Rejection
------

To evaluate whether the 3D-SLIP bounding gait exhibited a similar response to lateral and yaw disturbances as seen in Greco and Koditschek, 2023, we subjected the robot to aggressive pushes while it was bounding.  It handled these perturbations well, quickly resuming the nominal bounding motion without resorting to a dedicated recovery behavior.

The gait also demonstrated robustness to unanticipated variations in terrain, including unexpected slopes and dropoffs.  These results suggest that the 3D SLIP bounding gait retains a significant basin of attraction similar to the slot-hopper bounding gait analyzed in De and Koditschek, 2018.

Future Work
======

The 3D SLIP bounding gait exhibited some limitations on terrain that differed significantly from the flat ground on which it was tuned.  For example, when climbing steep hills or traversing very soft terrain, the hoping controller was unable to impart enough energy to continue the gait, and the movement would stall.  We anticipate that retuning the hopping controller to stabilize a higher-energy limit cycle would enable it to overcome these obstacles, and that a simple adaptive control scheme could detect these conditions through proprioception and adjust the controller accordingly.  Additionally, traversing steep slopes induced a significant offset in the roll direction, occasionally causing the robot to lose its balance.  We expect that adjusting the anchoring controller to incorporate information from the IMU can mitigate this behavior.  Finally, future work will take the general framework explored here and apply it to transitional maneuvers, generating a more expansive suite of behaviors for agile locomotion in 3D space.
